On-Time Delivery Transformed: 62% to 96% in One Year

The Situation

Chronic delivery failures had become the defining characteristic of this operation in its customers' eyes. At 62% on-time delivery, more than one in three shipments was late — triggering costly expediting, damaging customer relationships, and consuming management time in constant escalation cycles rather than improvement work.

The problem had existed long enough that it had been normalized. "We're a make-to-order operation" and "our customers have unpredictable demand" had become the accepted explanations. A fresh root cause investigation told a very different story.

Root Cause Investigation

Rather than accepting the narrative, a structured 8D investigation was launched alongside a full value stream map of the order-to-ship process. The goal was to understand not just that deliveries were late, but where in the process lateness was created and why.

The VSM revealed three primary root causes that accounted for the majority of late shipments:

01
Scheduling Inconsistency

Production schedules were being built weekly but modified multiple times per day in response to expedite requests, with no formal prioritization criteria. The result was a constantly shifting schedule that operators couldn't rely on and supervisors couldn't enforce. High-priority orders were frequently bumped by the loudest escalation, not the most important customer need.

02
Unclear Accountability Structure

When a delivery was at risk, it was unclear who owned the resolution. Customer service, production, and shipping each had partial visibility but no single owner of the delivery commitment. Problems were identified late and escalated slowly because nobody had clear authority to act.

03
Upstream Supply Variability

A subset of key components had inconsistent supplier lead times that were not reflected in the production schedule. When components arrived late, the schedule had no buffer and no response protocol — orders simply fell behind with no early warning.

"The delivery problem wasn't a capacity problem. It was a visibility, accountability, and discipline problem. Once we could see it clearly, fixing it was straightforward."

The Solution: Visibility, Accountability, and Discipline

The corrective actions addressed each root cause directly:

Scheduling Discipline

Implemented a frozen schedule window — the production sequence for the next 48 hours could not be changed without supervisor approval and documentation of the business reason. Expedite requests were funneled through a single intake process and prioritized against objective criteria rather than escalation pressure. Schedule adherence became a tracked daily metric visible to all.

Tiered Accountability Meetings

Established a three-tier daily meeting cadence: a 10-minute floor huddle at shift start reviewing that day's delivery commitments, a 20-minute cross-functional meeting at mid-morning identifying any at-risk orders, and a daily end-of-day review of next-day readiness. Each meeting had a clear owner, a standard agenda, and an escalation path. Problems surfaced hours earlier than before.

Visual Management Boards

Installed physical delivery status boards at each production cell showing the day's order commitments, current status (on track / at risk / late), and the name of the person responsible for each at-risk order. Visibility changed behavior — nobody wanted their name next to a red status on the floor board.

Supplier Performance Scorecards

Built a simple weekly supplier scorecard tracking on-time delivery by component and supplier. Shared it in monthly supplier reviews. For the three suppliers responsible for the most schedule disruptions, initiated formal corrective action requests and established safety stock buffers while lead time reliability was addressed.

Results

Measured Outcomes — 12 Months Post-Implementation
  • On-time delivery improved from 62% to 96% — a 34 percentage point gain sustained over multiple quarters
  • Expediting costs reduced significantly as emergency freight and overtime driven by late orders declined
  • Customer escalations related to delivery dropped by more than 80%
  • Schedule adherence (production vs. plan) improved from approximately 70% to 94%
  • Supplier on-time delivery for the three targeted components improved from 71% to 91% within six months

Key Lessons

  • Delivery problems are usually system problems. The root causes here had nothing to do with capacity and everything to do with visibility, process discipline, and accountability clarity.
  • Visual management changes behavior faster than reporting. Putting delivery status on a floor board had an immediate effect on urgency and ownership that no dashboard report had ever produced.
  • Tiered meetings work when they're short and standard. The daily cadence only succeeded because each meeting was 10–20 minutes, had a fixed agenda, and ended with clear action owners. Longer, looser meetings would have been abandoned within weeks.
  • Suppliers need feedback to improve. The three targeted suppliers weren't underperforming out of indifference — they simply hadn't received consistent, data-driven feedback. Scorecards and regular reviews changed the relationship.
